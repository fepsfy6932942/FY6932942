The Drag Reduction System in Formula 1

Abstract

The use of DRS has had a significant impact on the style that racing is conducted in modern Formula 1. The system was put in place to give the following driver an advantage which aids them in making their way past the driver ahead. The goal of this paper is to dig into the mechanics and aerodynamics behind the system while also comparing the system’s performance on different cars as well as tracks. The data used will be coming from telemetry readings gathered by the cars when DRS was in use. The results show that the system gives the driver the advantage in terms of straight-line speed, although it can seem dangerous to be used for the corner apex. Overall, the system performs up to its goal of giving the following driver the advantage.

Introduction

Formula 1 is the place where top level engineering meets daring driving skill. Cars are built specifically to be efficient and fast around a circuit for as long as possible. It is common to see races where overtakes are minimal, giving drivers no chance to get past the driver ahead. The most implemented way to overtake a driver is to get the opposing driver to make a mistake or to get on their bumper and use their slipstream to beat them to the corner. This slipstream effect became very minimal after the cars were updated aerodynamically in 2009. This made the car create a strong wake after it had driven through the air. This meant that getting close enough to use slipstream was a lot more dangerous than before. Cars were losing almost 50% of downforce when in the wake of another car.

Drivers were relying on tire degradation and pit stops for overtaking. This led to more processional races which became widely despised by fans. Their cries were heard by the FIA who worked tirelessly to find a solution. The 2010 season ended with just 547 overtakes in comparison to the season after, where overtaking numbers rose to 821.

How Wings Work

A Formula 1 car is like a plane flipped upside down. Rather than creating lift to fly, the wings generate downforce to push the car into the track, improving grip and cornering speed. The rear wing works by manipulating airflow over two elements: the main plane and the flap. When angled steeply, these surfaces increase pressure above and reduce pressure below, generating downforce. But downforce comes with drag—a force that resists forward motion and limits top speed.

The Drag Reduction System (DRS) changes the wing’s aerodynamic profile by raising the flap up to 50 mm. This reduces drag, decreases downforce, and increases straight-line speed by up to 15–20 km/h depending on track layout. However, because downforce is essential in corners, DRS is restricted to designated zones and cannot be used freely.

Why DRS Was Introduced

Before DRS, Formula 1 struggled with cars being unable to follow closely due to turbulent air produced by high-downforce wings. Drivers lost grip when approaching another car, making overtakes extremely difficult. In 2010, the average number of overtakes per race dropped so low that many fans and analysts called the racing “processional.” The FIA needed a mechanism that restored the slipstream effect safely without altering car design philosophy too drastically.

DRS was introduced in 2011 as a controlled way to reduce drag for the trailing car, giving them an incremental speed advantage that could convert close racing into an overtaking opportunity. While controversial, the system did increase overtaking frequency and helped address stagnation in on-track action.

Discussion

The effectiveness of DRS varies dramatically depending on the circuit. Tracks such as Monza, Baku, and Spa-Francorchamps—with long straights—maximize its impact. Technical circuits like Monaco or Hungaroring see limited benefit because straights are too short or too narrow for effective passes.

Telemetry data indicates that DRS typically provides a 12–20 km/h top-speed increase, but this depends on rear-wing angle, car philosophy, and engine mode. The downside is predictability—many fans argue overtakes produced via DRS are “too easy,” reducing the skill required. On the other hand, drivers note that without DRS, modern downforce levels would again make overtaking nearly impossible.

Conclusion

DRS fundamentally reshaped modern Formula 1 by re-enabling overtaking in an era defined by aerodynamic sensitivity. While not perfect, and sometimes producing overtakes that appear artificial, it successfully addresses the core problem of turbulent air preventing wheel-to-wheel racing. As regulations evolve toward ground-effect cars that allow closer following, DRS may eventually be phased out. For now, it remains one of the most influential innovations of the hybrid era.
